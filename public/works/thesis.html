Index 01 — Academic — TU Delft, 2015

Liminal
Thresholds

A Master of Architecture thesis exploring the threshold condition — the narrow, often unresolved zone where public ownership gives way to private use. Tested across a sequence of watercolor sections drawn through a reclaimed waterfront edge, the project argues that thresholds deserve to be designed as spaces in their own right, not as the leftover gap between two more important rooms.

Fig. 01 — Watercolor section, reclaimed waterfront edge

Research Question
What does it mean to design a threshold deliberately — as occupied space — rather than treat it as the residue left behind once the "real" rooms have been drawn?
Site
A reclaimed industrial waterfront edge undergoing a slow transition from working harbour to public promenade, with adjacent parcels in mixed public and private hands.
Methodology
Eleven hand-drawn watercolor sections, each testing a different threshold condition — stepped, layered, porous, gated — against the same 40-metre stretch of edge, supported by precedent mapping across six European waterfront cities.
Outcome
A working vocabulary of five threshold types later carried into early-career adaptive-reuse and waterfront commissions, where the same question — who owns this six metres, and when — kept resurfacing.
